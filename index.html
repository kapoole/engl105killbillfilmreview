Chapter One

Introduction

' '"Revenge is a dish best served cold." - Old Klingon Prover ' illustrates the idea that revenge is better after a long time of waiting. The longer the revenge builds the better the revenge. This 'Old Klingon Proverb' sets the stage to Kill Bill telling the audience exactly what to expect. Kill Bill was divided into two films, Vol. I (2003) and Vol. II (2004), both written and directed by Quentin Tarantino. A pregnant and unnamed Bride (Uma Thurman) barely survives the massacre of her wedding rehearsal by five members of the Deadly Viper Assassination Squad. The Bride is left in a coma for four years.

She awakens from her coma to find herself no longer pregnant and with revenge on her mind. he Bride creates a kill list of the five people responsible for the massacre and sets out to serve some cold revenge. Tarantino used chapters in order to separate the story into smaller pieces; however, these pieces are delivered to the audience out of order. By developing the narrative in this manner, the ending is not meant to catch the audience off guard since the ending of the move is not the ending of the storyline. In this sense, the audience already knows what happens.

How can a movie establish tension if the audience knows the result? Tarantino shifts the tension-building role from the plot and lets the music, violence, references to a variety of genres, jumps in the timeline take full rein. This shift changes the audience’s question from ‘What is going to happen?’ to ‘How is it going to happen?’. Kill Bill is founded on a simplistic plot of revenge but enhances the storytelling in order to keep the audience engaged and excited rather than surprised.

Chapter Two

A Story Told but Where Is the Plot

The plot of Kill bill is extremely simplistic. Indivdual feels wronged; said individual enacts revenge. The plot is even physically written in the movie in the form of the Death List Five. "The plot is as linear as the to-do list that drives it" (Orr). In this sense, there is no surprising moment in the plot. The audience is not surprised when The Bride kills O-ren since the plot continues after this moment. The audience is not surprised that The Bride makes it to Bill since the plot said she would get here. This movie doesn't contain spoilers in relation to the plot because the audience is told that The Bride kills Bill (it's the name of the movie). "If you think I have given away plot details, you think there can be doubt about whether the heroine survives the first half of a two-part action movie, and should seek help" (Ebert). The audience doesn't wonder, "Who is going to win this fight?". Thus, the role of tension is not held by the plot.

Similarly to how the plot is physically labelled in the movie, the narrative is labelled by chapters. The overall plot was divided into chapters which is how the story is being delivered to the audience. However, the chapters are out of order. The movie illustrates this out of sequence fashion clearly from the beginning with Chapter One having the subtitle 2, a reference to the 2 on The Bride's kill list. This dichotomoy of a linear plot told in a non-linear fashion excites the audience by keeping the audience aware of the ending but in the dark about previous events. Without a non-linear narrative, the plot by itself is too dry to entertain. " Tarantino's Tarantinoesque decision to present the killing of Vernita Green out of sequence feels more like obligation than inspiration" (Orr) . The non-linear deliverence of the plot is one of the ways that Kill Bill creates tension and excitement from the narrative.

Chapter Three

Revenge With a Spoonful Ton of Violence
*graphic warning*

"One of the reasons that fight directing can even exist as a profession is that every choreographed fight is necessarily narrative. There is always a story implicit in the way a cinematic or theatrical fight’s events unfold" (Anderson). Kill Bill's narrative takes on more responsibility for the tension and excitment, so the violence is used as a narrative tool in order to keep the audience engaged and awake. Tarantino excites the audience through the violence. The violence is able to supplement this responsibility by being specifically over-the-top and ridiculous.

The ridiculous nature of the violence in Kill Bill creates surreal fight scenes that defy realism. Many of the fights in Kill Bill include moments that are physically impossible. One clear example is the spraying of blood from wounds as it sprays like a fountain which is extremely dramaticized. Also the movement by the individuals in the movie are also not grounded in reality such as Pai Mei standing on a sword, fighters jumping over 10 feet in the air, and The Bride punching through 6 feet of soil. This exageration of violence and gore serves to amp up and entertain the audience contrasting the simplistic plot. "Here, as elsewhere, the fight constitutes the plot. Even such 'brutality,'' however, is not 'gratuitous' in the sense of simply superfluous movement. Nor does violence provide an 'excuse' for a display of sexualized bodies. Rather, this fight and others like it are the very reason that audiences watch the film in the first place. The story itself unfolds and revelations about the characters are revealed through these violent actions" (Anderson).

Chapter Four

Myriad of Genres

" 'One of the first statements Quentin made was that he wanted each chapter of the script to feel like a reel from a different film,' Richardson recalls. 'He wanted to move in and out of the various signature styles of all these genres - Western, melodrama, thriller, horror. He had an absolute knowledge of what he wanted each sequence to look like' " (Pavlus).
